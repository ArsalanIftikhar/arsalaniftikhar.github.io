Documentation and visibility controls that protect delivery

Short practical notes on the minimum artefacts I use so BI delivery stays governed, supportable, and decision-ready.

Where I apply this

BI workstreams spanning discovery, semantic design, reporting build, release, and handover.

What problem this avoids

KPI drift, undocumented logic, single-person dependency, and unclear ownership after go-live.

Why it matters in practice

Without visibility artefacts, teams lose trust in numbers and support overhead rises quickly.

Minimum artefacts by stage

  • Discovery: scope/outcomes note, decision owners, non-goals.
  • KPI governance: concise metric dictionary and change log.
  • Model design: data-flow diagram, semantic relationship notes, grain definitions.
  • Validation: reconciliation results and exception triage paths.
  • Release: runbook, release notes, and support ownership map.

How this supports trusted reporting

  • Keeps metric definitions explicit and reviewable.
  • Makes lineage easier to explain in senior review conversations.
  • Improves handover quality so BAU support is stable.

Common failure pattern

Teams often document only at the end. The better pattern is lightweight artefacts updated through delivery, so decisions are visible while they are still easy to correct.

What this demonstrates

Delivery maturity in BI is not just model quality; it is control of definitions, visibility of decisions, and operationally reliable handover.
