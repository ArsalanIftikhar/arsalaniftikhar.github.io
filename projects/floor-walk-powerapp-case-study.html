Floor Walk Power App Case Study

Process digitisation and governance-first delivery to replace inconsistent floor walk capture with structured operational data and reliable trend visibility.

Context

Operational floor walk checks were being captured manually with inconsistent structure and limited historical comparability.

Stakeholders

Site operations leads, area supervisors, continuous improvement partners, and BI consumers tracking issue trends.

My role & scope

Senior MI Analyst delivering process design inputs, data model standards, governance controls, and reporting outputs.

Stack

Power Apps (Canvas), SharePoint List, Power Query, Power BI semantic model, Power BI reporting.

Controls

Required fields, controlled categories, location naming standards, timestamp capture, and ownership/accountability fields.

Outcome

Improved visibility of recurring floor issues, reduced manual collation, and better action tracking through standardised capture.

Problem & context

Floor walk findings were previously recorded in ad-hoc formats across teams. This created inconsistency in issue labels, location naming, and ownership detail, making it difficult to compare periods or identify repeat problems reliably. Reporting was possible, but heavily manual and prone to interpretation drift.

Design goals

  • Standardisation: establish one practical structure for issue capture across shifts and areas.
  • Adoption: keep data entry simple enough for regular operational use.
  • Accountability: retain clear ownership and actionability at record level.
  • Governance: protect data quality so trends can be trusted over time.

Solution

The solution used a Power Apps Canvas app for guided floor walk capture, writing to a SharePoint List with standardised fields. Power Query shaped and validated list data for analytics usage before loading into a Power BI semantic model and dashboards for trend review.

Data quality & governance controls

  • Required fields for issue type, area/location, severity context, and owner.
  • Controlled category values to reduce reporting noise from free-text variants.
  • Consistent location naming conventions to prevent duplicate location labels.
  • Automated timestamp capture to support daily/weekly/monthly trend slices.
  • Ownership and status fields to improve follow-up accountability.

Adoption approach

Adoption was handled pragmatically: keep the form concise, align fields to existing floor walk behaviour, and gather supervisor feedback during pilot usage before broader rollout. The objective was reliable repeat usage, not feature-heavy complexity. Short guidance notes and clear field labels supported consistency without over-engineering training.

Reporting outcomes

With standardised capture in place, reporting shifted from reactive collation to trend-led insight. Typical views included:

  • Recurring issue patterns by aisle, zone, or product family.
  • Hotspot identification where repeated observations clustered by location.
  • Repeat categories across shifts and teams, highlighting process control gaps.
  • Time-based trends showing whether issue frequency was stabilising or re-emerging.

Outcomes

  • Improved visibility into floor walk findings through consistent historical records.
  • Reduced manual effort previously required to consolidate operational checks.
  • Standardised process and data definitions across contributing teams.
  • More actionable review conversations supported by clearer trend evidence.

Next steps

  • Introduce SLA-style follow-up tracking for higher-severity categories.
  • Add simple exception alerts for repeated issues above agreed thresholds.
  • Periodically review controlled category sets to keep definitions current.
